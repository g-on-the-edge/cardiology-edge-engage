Clinical Resource Model

How it Works - Summary

The Clinical Resource Model is a data-driven tool designed to precisely quantify the staff, equipment, and space required to operate the service line. It uses CPT codes as the fundamental building blocks to represent every piece of work, providing a foundational blueprint for understanding our current operational needs and for strategically planning for the future.

Piece of Work

The model's foundation is the complete list of CPT codes performed across the service line. Each CPT code is mapped to the specific resources required for its execution: staff time, equipment, and space.

A key strength of this model is its flexibility in defining a complete "piece of work," like a patient appointment or a complex procedure. In some cases, this involves aggregating multiple CPT codes from various teams. In other instances, a single CPT code can serve as a proxy to represent the entire scope of work, even if it doesn't traditionally describe every component. For example, a complex multi-disciplinary procedure involves work from both surgical teams and interventional specialists. The model is designed to capture all this associated work—whether triggered by multiple CPTs or a single representative one—to ensure the full resource picture is accurately represented.

Note: sometimes it may be necessary to pull in additional data pieces to supplement CPT codes (in basket messages, etc).

Quantifying Different Workflows

The model also accounts for the different nature of clinical work.

  • Linear Workflows: For roles like physicians, work is often linear and scheduled (e.g., patient appointments, planned procedures, reading scans). This work is more easily tied directly to CPT codes.
  • Request-Driven Workflows: In contrast, roles like nursing often involve non-linear, request-driven work. Their day is a dynamic mix of responding to patient calls, managing inbox messages, and coordinating with providers. The model quantifies this work by analyzing patterns and volumes of these requests, ensuring this less predictable but critical work is accurately resourced.

Two Views: Current State vs. Future State

1. Current State Model

This model provides a data-backed snapshot of our operations as they exist today. It quantifies the resources needed based on our current workflows, roles, and processes, including any existing inefficiencies. For example, if nurses are currently performing tasks that could be delegated, the Current State Model accurately reflects the nursing time required for those tasks under the present design. It answers the question: "What does it take to run the service line right now?"

Example: Current State Nursing Work

Phone Calls: 22/day × 8 min × 22 days = 64.5 hours
In-Basket: 45/day × 4 min × 22 days = 66.0 hours
Scheduling: 15/day × 5 min × 22 days = 27.5 hours
Total Monthly Nurse Time: 158.0 hrs
(≈ 0.91 FTE)

2. Future State Model

This is a strategic and aspirational version of the model. It redesigns resource allocation by synthesizing insights from a comprehensive discovery process, including staff interviews, direct observations, and care model research. It also incorporates best practices from internal and external experts, such as those within the Department of Nursing. Every proposed change in the Future State Model is directly mapped to a pain point or theme identified by our teams. This creates a clear line of sight from an acknowledged problem to a data-driven solution, answering the question: "What resources would we need if we operated in an ideal, optimized state?"

Example: Future State Nursing Work (Reallocated Scheduling)

Phone Calls: 22/day × 8 min × 22 days = 64.5 hours
In-Basket: 45/day × 4 min × 22 days = 66.0 hours
Scheduling: 15/day × 5 min × 22 days = 27.5 hours
Reallocated
New Total Monthly Time: 130.5 hrs
(≈ 0.75 FTE)

What The Resource Model Delivers

Ultimately, the model is a powerful decision-making engine that provides clear, actionable insights.

  • Precise Resource Quantification: It tells us exactly how many staff members of each role, what specific equipment, and how much clinical space are needed to meet patient demand.
  • Data-Driven Decision-Making: It replaces assumptions and anecdotal evidence with objective data, allowing leadership to make confident, defensible decisions about hiring, capital purchases, and space allocation.
  • Dynamic Scenario Planning: The model is not a static document; it's a flexible forecasting tool. Both the Current State and Future State versions can be used to model various scenarios by adjusting variables like patient volume. This allows us to answer critical strategic questions from either perspective, such as:
    • "What staffing increase is needed to support 3% annual growth over the next five years?"
    • "What resources are required to completely eliminate our current patient backlog in nine months?"
    • "How would our resource needs change if we shifted to a new care delivery strategy?"

Final Notes on Methodology

Even though the examples above are targeted at staffing, the same methodology is relevant to both space and equipment. Additionally, staffing and FTE need not be limited to operational staff only, it can also be extended to allotted leader resourcing.

Further, the methodology can feasibly be extended to encompass even non-clinical areas. All that would be required in such cases is identifying a different work driver metric around which to model allocation of resources.

Critically, the resource model is built on a platform of commonly agreed-upon responsibilities, behaviors, and ultimately standard work. As more definition and alignment is added to this platform, the resource model becomes more and more accurate.
